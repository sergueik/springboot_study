In modern "HR" or corporate language, you could describe it like this:

Martin Denny = founder, visionary, public face, bandleader, brand.
Arthur Lyman = key technical contributor, principal "engineer" of the signature sound.

Lyman wasn't just a session musician who played what he was told. He was an exceptionally creative vibraphonist and percussionist, and many music historians credit him with shaping the sonic identity of the early Denny recordings. The famous bird calls, jungle atmosphere, shimmering vibraphone, and intricate percussion interplay were all integral to what made Exotica sound unlike anything else at the time.

However, I'd stop short of saying Denny was merely the "business guy." Denny:

  * chose the repertoire
  * arranged much of the music
  * conceived the "exotic" concept
  * led the band

and was responsible for the overall artistic direction.

So the Apple analogy is suggestive but not exact.


Among casual listeners, Martin Denny is still the name that most people recognize. His albums are the gateway into exotica.

Among serious listeners, collectors, and audiophiles, it's quite common to hear something like:

"If you like Denny, don't stop there—listen to Arthur Lyman"

In fact, many enthusiasts end up preferring Lyman's recordings because they often feature:

  * more virtuosic vibraphone playing
  * a slightly more natural, less theatrical sound
  * excellent stereo recording quality (especially on HiFi Records)
  *  stronger emphasis on musicianship than on the novelty of the "jungle" effects

It's a bit like this:

  * Martin Denny introduces you to the genre
  * Arthur Lyman often becomes the artist you return to repeatedly if you're listening closely

That doesn't diminish Denny's achievement.
Without Denny's vision, exotica as a recognizable style might never have become a mainstream phenomenon. But once the style exists, someone who is especially interested in sound quality, percussion, vibraphone, and recording fidelity may indeed gravitate toward Lyman's catalog.

Your phrasing of a "true sound addict" is apt. Such a listener might smile knowingly and say, "Yes, Denny is wonderful—but have you heard Arthur Lyman's recordings?" That's not an uncommon sentiment among exotica enthusiasts and audiophiles.
